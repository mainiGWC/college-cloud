Extracurriculars

What defines an "extracurricular"?

Although they may sound complex and intimidating, extracurriculars encompass almost anything that’s isn’t required for credit and that isn’t simply for your own benefit, like playing video games. And the good news is, you probably do a few already! They really can be anything: clubs, sports, community service, employment, academic teams — the list goes on and on. As long as the activity involves your contribution to others, it’s pretty safe to call it an extracurricular.



Why are they so important?

Extracurriculars are a vital component of college applications; along with test scores, grades, and essays, they’re just one more way to show colleges your unique abilities and interests. Whether or not you have the luxury of pursuing sports or interest clubs, colleges really want to know how you spend your time outside of school. Outside involvement (especially long-term) implies dedication and showcases your hunger to make an impact on the world around you. Extracurriculars are an amazing asset to your own life, as well; activities like volunteering teach gratefulness and humility, and academic teams can further your knowledge of potential interests and set you ahead in those subjects. Not to mention, they’re the perfect way to meet new friends and make connections that may come in handy down the line. Overall, extracurricular activities can be some of your best friends in high school, and we highly recommend you partake in plenty.


How to get started:

Now, you’re probably wondering exactly how you can get involved in your community. Well, there are three main steps you should consider taking to decide how to proceed.

  1. Taking some time to research what organizations are available around you should be your first thought. Many cities have parks or libraries with bulletin boards of activities, and almost all middle and high schools will have clubs, academic teams, and sports teams enumerated on their website. When researching, try to consider some communities you already belong to. Do you go to church? Are you interested in math? Maybe debate is more your thing? How about running? Thinking about what you already do or are interested in will be extremely helpful in narrowing down your searches. And remember: these do not have to be in-person activities! There are plenty of remote volunteer opportunities such as blanket-making or card-crafting that can be done right from the comfort of your own home.

  2. Now that you’ve researched, try to make a list of at least three possible activities, but ideally as many as you can. There’s a good chance some of them won’t take, and at least you’ll have all your other options to back you up! Note: College applications have anywhere from 10-20 slots for activities, so try to always be participating in a few so that you’ll accumulate many throughout high school!

  3. Your third move is to test out all the activities on your list. This is an absolutely essential step since there’s a good chance some of your activities won’t be your cup of tea, or that they don’t fit your schedule. Plus, this gives you a chance to gauge whether the community, team, or organization will be beneficial to you in the long term. Note: reflect on each activity after you’ve attended at least one (preferably two) meetings. ask yourself this: can i see myself continuing here long- term? Will this contribute to my life in a positive way, without taking away from my studies or personal life? If the answer is yes, I think you’ve got your extracurricular!
